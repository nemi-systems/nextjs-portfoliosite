There is a picture of knowing that almost no one defends out loud and almost everyone reaches for by default. In this picture, knowledge is reception. The world is already there, formed and finished, and knowing it is a matter of taking in what is sent — light into eyes, sound into ears, data into instruments, information into minds. The knower is a receiver. The known is a transmitter. The transaction is one-directional and zero-cost: the world is not changed by being known, and the knower incurs no debt for the knowing.

This picture is wrong, and the wrongness is not a technical curiosity. It has a moral architecture, and when the technical error is finally admitted, the moral architecture collapses with it. What takes its place is not a modest correction. It is a different account of what knowing is, and with it, a different account of what one owes to what one attends to.

### The receiver and its uses

The receiver picture is convenient in ways that are worth naming before arguing against it. If knowledge is reception, then observation is innocent. The camera takes no tax from the scene. The mind takes no tax from the fact. The ideal of the detached scientific observer, the journalist merely recording, the researcher merely describing — each of these inherits its dignity from the assumption that looking extracts nothing from what is looked at. Surveillance presents itself this way: we are only watching. Market research presents itself this way: we are only asking. Intelligence services, census takers, ethnographers, critics, even the nosy neighbor all draw on the same background conviction. The eye does not touch what it sees.

The word *objective* does a lot of work here. To be objective is to be absent from the account: to let the world speak, to remove the observer from the observation. The passive voice of scientific writing is the grammatical form of the receiver picture — *the solution was titrated*, *the data were collected* — and its rhetorical power comes from the shared sense that the best knowing is the kind that leaves the fewest fingerprints. Knowledge is purest when the knower has taken nothing and given nothing.

Hold this picture in mind. Not because it is especially sophisticated — it is not — but because it is what people fall back to when they are tired, and it is what institutions claim to be doing when they would prefer not to be held responsible for doing something else.

### Probes, not windows

![A single electromechanical transducer in cross-section at the center of a mirrored time-axis, readable as a microphone in one direction and as a speaker in the other](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_transducer_duality.png)

Consider measurement. Not as a philosophical abstraction but as something physical that a physical instrument does.

A microphone is a transducer — it converts pressure variations in air into voltage variations in a wire. Run the same device backward, push voltage through the wire, and you get a speaker: voltage variations become pressure variations in air. Microphone and speaker are not two instruments. They are one instrument read in two directions. The same is true of motors and generators, antennas for transmission and reception, strain gauges and piezoelectric actuators. Every transducer has this duality. There is no class of instrument that receives without being capable, in principle, of emitting. The categories are the same physics in opposite arrows of time.

This is not a quirk of engineering. It is a symptom. Measurement is not a window onto a state; it is a coupling to a state. To read the temperature of a liquid you put a probe in and let the probe reach equilibrium with the liquid — which means the probe took some of the liquid's heat. To weigh something you set it on a platform whose springs you then read; the platform pushed back on the weighed object. To see a thing you interpose a retina in the path of its photons and absorb them; the photons are gone after. Every act of reading a state requires a coupling, and every coupling is symmetric. You cannot measure without being measured. You cannot touch without being touched.

![Elevation cross-section of a cathedral-scale swimming pool with a single slender thermometer dropped in, annotated with concentric iso-temperature rings and a return-flow arrow marked as a thermometer's worth of degrees leaving the pool](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_thermometer_pool.png)

Classical mechanics made the receiver picture look like it held by letting the measuring apparatus be arbitrarily small relative to the thing measured, so that the return effect was negligible. A thermometer dropped into a swimming pool reads the pool's temperature to the usual precision without anyone noticing that the pool dropped a thermometer's worth of degrees. The fiction works as long as the scales permit it. But the fiction is a fiction, not a floor. Push the scales in the other direction — shrink the measured thing to atomic size, use probes whose quanta of interaction are comparable to the quanta of the thing being probed — and the return effect becomes quantitatively unignorable. What is called, misleadingly, the *observer effect* in quantum mechanics is not a mysterious feature of the quantum world. It is the ordinary feature of measurement that showed up first in the regime where it got large enough to notice.

![Side-view biological cross-section of an echolocating figure mid-click, fan of outgoing pulse-wavefronts and dashed returned echoes, paired with an inset of a saccading eye whose gaze-vector flicks between two fixation points](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_echolocation_probe.png)

The same pattern repeats up the ladder. Vision is not passive. The retina samples, the eyes saccade, the brain predicts and updates — perception is an active inference loop that emits predictions and reads errors. Echolocating animals hear their own clicks come back; the click is the probe and the return is the reading, and the animal is visible to anything else in earshot. Scent is inseparable from sniffing: to know a smell is to draw a pulse of air past the receptors, and the pulse is an emission. Every sensory system operates this way on inspection. The receiver picture described none of them accurately. It was always an idealization produced by not looking closely enough at what sensing is.

So: observation is a probe-loop. You send something in, something comes back, you update. There is no such thing as looking without, in some degree, touching. This is the technical claim, and it is unexceptionable at the level of the physics and the biology. What the rest of this essay asks is what follows if it is taken seriously when the probe is attention, and the probed is a person, a community, a market, or a world.

### Attention as intervention

![Overhead architectural cutaway of a panoptic watchtower at the center of a tick-marked grid of small figures, with downward measurement cones and upward feedback arrows showing the population reshaping itself under the gaze](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_goodhart_tower.png)

If every act of knowing is an emission into what is known, then the moral ledger has to be rewritten. The clean separation between *finding out* and *interfering* no longer holds at the level at which the ledger keeps books. Attention is intervention. Every sustained looking is a sustained loop-commitment. What you attend to, you change. What you refuse to attend to, you refuse to change.

This is not a metaphor and it is not a provocation. It is the everyday content of the claim that measurement is coupling, applied to cases where the thing measured is itself sensitive to being measured — which is to say, applied to nearly everything that is not a rock.

Consider the canonical test case: Goodhart's law. *When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.* This is usually filed under administrative wisdom, a rule of thumb for people who run organizations. It is in fact the degenerate form of the probe-loop claim, the form in which the transceivance of measurement has become so obvious that nobody can pretend otherwise. You watch a number; the people being numbered notice they are being watched; they change what they do; the number is now the signal of a different thing than it was. The probe has been emitted; the loop has closed; what was measured is now something else. Every institution that has ever been surprised by this was operating, without admitting it, under the receiver picture.

Extend the move. Surveillance is not neutral — not because it is ominous, though it often is, but because observation is not the kind of thing that can be neutral. The population that knows it is watched is a different population than the one that does not. The employee whose keystrokes are counted writes different keystrokes. The citizen who suspects a file exists on her conducts herself differently in the spaces she thinks the file reaches. None of this requires malice from the watchers. It is the ordinary consequence of sustained attention being a form of pressure. You cannot watch a large number of people for long without becoming part of what they are doing, and pretending otherwise is not modesty; it is confusion about what watching is.

Extend it further. A recommender system is not handing you content; it is running a probe-loop. Each click is an emission from you, often one you did not quite know you were making, into a coordinate system you cannot see. The system updates on the emission, which means the system *acts*, which means the world you next encounter is not the world you would have otherwise encountered. You are not the user of the system in the sense that you are the user of a hammer. You are the slow half of a transceiver pair, and the fast half is remaking the environment you were trying to navigate. The sense of being caught inside something after too long in one of these systems is not paranoia. It is an accurate proprioceptive response to being inside a loop whose other end cannot be reached at comparable speed.

![Exploded side-view clockwork gear-train: an enormous fine-toothed institutional wheel at high angular velocity meshing through a single brittle coupling tooth with a tiny slow individual wheel carrying one lone emission-arrow](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_gear_asymmetry.png)

Extend it one more step. The peculiar unease of modern bureaucratic life — that one is continuously sized up by entities one cannot size up back — is not exactly a feeling about inscrutability. It is a feeling about loop asymmetry. A credit bureau reads thousands of coordinates about you per day, updates its model, and emits signals that enter the world you act in: prices you see, offers you get, doors that open or do not. You read no coordinates of the credit bureau. You cannot update on it. You cannot emit back at anything like the same rate. The loop is closed at one end and open at the other. This is not surveillance in the melodramatic sense; it is the ordinary structure of a system that learns about you without being learnable by you. The precise name for the condition is *legible without recourse*. You are continuously measured by probes you cannot probe back, and whatever the probes are coming to know is also shaping what you now are, because the probe's outputs enter the environment you are trying to act within.

The discomfort people report about these systems is correct. The thing that stops the discomfort from becoming usable is naming it wrong. It is not that the systems know too much. It is that knowing is an action, and the action is running only one way.

### The ethics the receiver picture concealed

If looking is doing, then the pretense of neutral observation had always been doing work for certain kinds of actors. A journalist who claims only to report is at minimum claiming that reporting has no gradient along which the reported can slide. A researcher who claims only to describe is at minimum claiming that the described can be described without the describing becoming a new condition they have to live in. These claims are sometimes close enough to true to be useful, but they are always fictions, and they have always cost something. The question is only who carried the cost.

Under the receiver picture, the cost was paid and invisible. The community studied by an ethnographer was changed by the study; the study's findings were treated as describing the community, with no line-item for the change the study itself had introduced. The market measured by an indicator was shifted by the measurement; the indicator was treated as descriptive, with no line-item for the shift. The lover or friend or child scrutinized by a close observer was altered by the scrutiny; the observer reported what they saw, with no line-item for what they had made. In every case the emission was real and the bookkeeping was silent about it. Somebody metabolized the emission. It just wasn't the person holding the pen.

Naming the receiver picture as a picture — rather than as the way things are — makes this visible. The knower's debt to the known is not an add-on imposed by unusually sensitive ethics. It is a consequence of what knowing is. If reception is the wrong word for it, then the ethical categories that rode on the word have to be rebuilt. Innocence is not available for a probe-loop. Neutrality is not available for a coupling. Detachment is not available for an emission. What is available — what replaces all of these — is something like *accountability for the loops one is part of*.

### Which loops

The first response to this claim is almost always a retreat: if attention is intervention, then perhaps one should attend less, watch fewer things, keep to oneself. This is not wrong as a corrective to excess, but it misunderstands the structure. You do not get to be outside the loops by closing your eyes. Refusal of attention is also an action. The things you look away from go dark at whatever rate they would have gone dark without you, and the communities and projects and people you decline to measure are the ones your emissions no longer support. What you do not feed does not grow. What you do not probe does not stay definite.

So the question is not whether to emit, which is not a live option. The question is which loops to close and which to keep closing. Every sustained attention is a commitment of a probe-rate to a particular coordinate. Over time, the pattern of your emissions — what you read, who you listen to, whose work you use, what you measure, what you let yourself notice — is the pattern of what you are helping hold definite in the world. This is not a metaphysical claim. It is the transceivance claim run over weeks and years. The world you live in is downstream, in specifiable and often traceable ways, of which probe-loops you kept open.

Contemplative traditions have been saying versions of this for a very long time, and it is worth hearing them once the frame makes their claim cash out in ordinary physical terms. *What you attend to, you feed; what you feed, grows.* This reads as mysticism when it is separated from any account of why it should be true. Under the transceivance picture, it is just what attention is. Attention is a sustained probe-loop. A sustained probe-loop emits into its target. The emission shapes what the target next becomes. The target, now shaped, is what the next emission enters. The pattern compounds. This is not a special fact about meditation. It is an ordinary fact about being a creature whose looking is also doing, and who cannot therefore look at many things at once without consequence. The monastic's discipline of attention is a practice of loop-selection. So is everyone else's, just less consciously.

### The view from nowhere

![A crossed-out transmitter-receiver arrow supplanted by a dense lattice of coupled closed loops at varying diameter and coupling-rate, each loop tagged by geometry as a sustained probe-commitment](/assets/no-receiving/ai_img_chatgpt_loops_manifesto.png)

The final move is the one the argument has been walking toward from the first paragraph. The *view from nowhere* — the ideal of the observer who is not in the scene, whose knowing leaves no trace, whose objectivity is purchased by vanishing from the account — was always epistemologically incoherent. This has been clear in philosophy of science for at least a century; it has been clear in physics since the 1920s; it has been clear in anthropology since ethnographers started writing about what their presence had done. What has been less clearly said is the corollary. If the view from nowhere is incoherent, it is not only wrong; it is *not available*. It is not a thing one can aspire to and fall short of. It is not a thing one can approximate. It is a posture that describes no possible actor, and insisting on it has never produced modesty. It has produced unaccountability.

Replacing it is the work of a lifetime. It involves accepting that one is always in the scene, that one's looking is always doing, that every institution one participates in is running loops whose effects one is partly responsible for, and that the question *which loops am I closing* is not optional but continuous. It is not the question of a saint. It is the question of anyone who has understood what measurement is.

There is no receiving. There are only loops, closed at different rates by different couplings, and a lifetime of choices about which ones to keep closing. The first two words of the essay's title are the whole of its claim. The second two are the whole of its ethics.
