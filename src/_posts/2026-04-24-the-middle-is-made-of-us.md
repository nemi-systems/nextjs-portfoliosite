---
title: "The Middle Is Made of Us"
date: 2026-04-24
---

![A vertical split-frame architectural cross-section: on the left half, a tall tech-project tower rising upward through stacked strata, and on the right half, a descending humanities shaft drilling downward through reframed layers, the two excavations oriented toward each other but not meeting](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_hero.png)

Two great projects have been running for several centuries, each probing toward what it takes to be the ground of things, and they have not met.

The first — call it the tech project, though it is older than anything we now call tech — builds upward. From transistors to logic gates to instruction sets to operating systems to compilers to frameworks to models to agents. Each layer, once stable, becomes the primitive that subsequent work treats as given. You do not reason about gates when you are training a transformer; you reason about attention heads. You will not reason about attention heads much longer, because something will have abstracted over them. The tower rises by forgetting its lower floors — not abandoning them, but treating them as floor rather than as structure. This forgetting is how the project works. Without it nothing would ever get built.

The second project — call it the humanities, though it includes phenomenology, theology, psychoanalysis, literature, anthropology, and their hybrids — drills downward. From consciousness to experience to language to embodiment to culture to material conditions. Each layer, once reached, becomes the terrain that earlier frames had taken for bedrock and later frames will treat as constructed. You do not reason about the Cartesian subject when you are doing affect theory; you reason about affective intensities and the socialities that constitute them. The shaft descends by reframing its earlier strata — not abandoning them, but treating them as artifact rather than as ground.

Both projects produce strata. Neither produces a meeting.

This is the first thing to notice, and the one most obscured by the rhetoric of each side. Bottom-up and top-down are not two halves of a handshake. They are two excavations that happen to be oriented toward each other but are digging in different substrates, with different tools, measuring different quantities, and — crucially — building different artifacts as they go.

A brief word, here, for the reader who has not been following the framework this essay is part of. I am going to use a picture in which every act of knowing is an emission into what is known. A probe, not a window. Knowing is not the passive reception of pre-existing information but a coupling between a knower and what the knower acts upon, in which the knower's action is itself part of what then appears as knowledge. This is not meant as a controversial metaphysical posture; it is meant as a description of the actual structure of observation, which even a moment's attention to instrumentation — microscopes, microphones, eyes, questionnaires, algorithms — makes evident. What you measure, you touch. What you touch, you change. The record of the touching is what the knowledge is made of.

Once knowing has this shape, two consequences follow that will matter for the rest of the essay. First, every sustained inquiry leaves residue. The probe-loop, in closing, marks both the probed and the probe; the mark on the probed is what we call the finding; the mark on the probe is the inquirer's transformed frame. Neither mark disappears. Second, inquiries run by communities over time stabilize the shared space in which their successors work. Today's hard-won abstractions become tomorrow's primitives; tomorrow's critiques of those abstractions become the day-after's clichés. What a field inherits is the sediment of prior closures.

With this in place the two-directions image sharpens. The tech project is a tower of closed loops, each abstraction load-bearing for the one above it. The humanities project is a descending shaft of opened loops, each reframing un-grounding what earlier work took for given. They are not converging on a shared floor. They are each producing strata. And the strata, crucially, do not get cleaned up.

This is the middle soup.

![An archaeological stratigraphic cross-section of the middle soup, with visible sediment layers of abandoned-but-residual frameworks rendered as fossil strata](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_sediment_stratum.png)

When a new layer stabilizes on either side, the old layers do not dissolve. They persist — as vocabularies, as institutions, as habits of thought, as inherited commitments people built careers and identities around, as training materials for the next generation, as the furniture of general discourse. Freudian metapsychology is still in the soup. Symbolic AI is still in the soup. Behaviorism is in the soup. Phrenology's ghost is in the soup — when we ask whether certain kinds of cognition correlate with certain brain regions we are, in a very real sense, asking the phrenologist's question with better instruments. The humoral theory left residues in how we still talk about temperament; *sanguine* and *melancholic* are phrases used comfortably by people who do not otherwise believe there are four bodily fluids determining character. Every abandoned structure was once load-bearing for someone, and abandonment is never total. The sediment accumulates.

And the sediment is not neutral. It shapes which moves are thinkable by those working above or beneath it. A contemporary philosopher of mind has to negotiate the ghost of behaviorism whether or not she endorses any of its claims; a contemporary machine learning researcher has to negotiate the ghost of symbolic AI, has to know its history in order to know what she is not doing. The middle is not empty space between the two projects. It is populated. It is the condition of working at all.

Now I want to sit with the other half of the intuition, if you are a reader who has had it — the one that says there is, nonetheless, a direction these two projects are facing, and that if either of them actually completed its task, something strange would happen.

Here is the strange thing, compressed. If the tech project completed its mapping — if every abstractum and every phenomenon in the knowable universe were cleanly represented in some finished model — the model would not be a picture of the universe. It would be a picture that included the picturer. Because the probe-loop, at completion, cannot exclude its own origin. The map that excludes the mapmaker is not finished; it is a map with one very important region missing. A map that includes the mapmaker has the mapmaker inside it, which makes the whole thing recursive in a way that does not terminate in any ordinary object. The recursion terminates, if it terminates at all, in whatever was doing the mapping — which is not an item on the map, because it is what makes maps possible. Call it consciousness, call it the clearing, call it the fact that there is something it is like to be doing any of this. The naming is downstream; the structure is the point.

![A three-panel apophatic plate: a via-negativa cathedral nave with an empty altar-frame, a Buddhist dependent-origination wheel, and an Advaita seer-cannot-be-seen diagram, each refusing positive determination of the ground](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_apophatic_triptych.png)

The apophatic traditions have been naming this for millennia, under whatever vocabularies they had available. The *via negativa* says it by refusing every positive determination of God — God is not this, not that, not any coordinate the namer can name, because the namer is the thing ultimately in question. Buddhist emptiness says it by pointing at the dependent origination of every phenomenon and noting that the dependence never terminates in a self-existing ground — only in relation, and the relational fabric is not itself a relatum. Advaita says it more directly: the seer cannot be seen, and what cannot be seen but by which all seeing occurs is what the tradition points at. A sufficiently complete science and a sufficiently complete apophatic theology are not opposing projects. They are two probes into the same loop from different points, and at completion they both arrive at the thing that is not an object in the loop but is what makes the loop a loop.

This is the sense in which the road that runs directly away from God also leads to God. Not because of any mystical secret, but because the road is a probe-loop, and all probe-loops close on what was probing. The accelerationist vision and the mystic's insight share a terminus that neither of them has reached, and the sharing of that terminus is not a coincidence; it is the shape of probing itself.

![A recursive cartographer's plate: an overhead drafting desk holds a mapmaker-figure bent over a wide map, and on that map a smaller mapmaker sits at a smaller desk drawing a still smaller map, the recursion spiraling inward without terminating](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_mapmaker_recursion.png)

But — and this is the payload — neither project is anywhere near completion. And the middle soup is precisely what you get when incomplete projects mistake their intermediate strata for the terminus.

![A paired survey-plate diptych of premature closure: a bottom-up worker atop a neural-substrate stratum declaring it the floor, and a top-down worker at a phenomenal-givenness stratum declaring it the ceiling, each closing the loop early](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_premature_closure_diptych.png)

The failure mode on both sides has the same structure. The bottom-up worker reaches a stratum in which consciousness cannot be found and, instead of concluding that the probe has not reached deep enough, concludes that there is nothing to reach — consciousness an illusion, qualia a confusion, the hard problem a bad framing. The top-down worker reaches a stratum in which phenomenal givenness seems irreducible and, instead of concluding that the analysis has not reached far enough, concludes that there is nothing further — substrate irrelevant, physicalism a category error, matter a post-hoc rationalization of experience. Each worker has closed a loop early. Each worker has announced arrival at a convergence that the respective project has not actually reached. And each announcement is itself soup-production — it adds another stratum to the middle that later work will have to wade through or clear.

The AI doomsday cultist who says the sufficiently large model will awaken is treating a bottom-up stratum as the top. The spiritual bypasser who says there is only awareness and all else is illusion is treating a top-down stratum as the bottom. Both are adding to the soup. Not because they are wrong in any simple sense — the sufficiently large model might awaken, and it might well be awareness all the way — but because the announcement is premature, and prematurity is what produces residue the next worker must contend with. The eliminativist and the perennialist, seen from far enough away, are performing the same move from opposite sides: each is taking a stratum they reached and calling it the floor, or the ceiling, and declining to probe further because declaring is easier than continuing.

There is a further move here, and it belongs to a register the earlier parts of this essay have only implied.

Once you take seriously that probing leaves residue — that your emissions become the soup the next worker has to wade through — the question of how to work stops being only methodological. It becomes a question about what I will call *second perception*.

![A field-diagram of second perception: a central inquirer-figure among a lattice of peer-figures, thin probe-loops reaching from the inquirer outward and second-perception return-arrows curving back from the field's response](/assets/the-middle-is-made-of-us/ai_img_chatgpt_second_perception_field.png)

The first closure of a probe-loop is epistemic: the probe returns information, the inquirer has their result. The second closure is different. It is the inquirer perceiving what their emission did to the field it entered — whether the signal landed, whom it disturbed, what shape of thought it made newly possible, what shape it quietly occluded. This is not a rhetorical nicety. It is the only way to know what one has actually contributed, because emissions are underdetermined; only the field's response specifies them in retrospect. You do not know what you said until you see it heard.

Most failure modes of modern inquiry are failures of second perception. The paper published and never looked at again to see what it did to the discourse. The framework announced and defended and not tested against the souring of its concepts in the hands of its second-generation practitioners. The algorithm deployed without watching what it did to the community it was supposed to serve. Each is epistemically complete — the first closure has happened, the probe has returned, the author has their result — and disciplinarily unfinished. The residue is what these unclosed second loops leave behind.

The discipline, if there is one, is to keep probing without declaring arrival, and to watch what the probing does to the field. Treat each stratum as stable but non-terminal. Emit into the shared space and attend to what your emissions do there. Accept that even so — even with the most scrupulous second perception — some of what you produce will become someone else's soup. This is not a failure of the work; it is the structural fact of work conducted in a community over time. Every workable framework is going to become somebody's residue.

The goal is not a soup-free epistemology. The goal is to not add unnecessarily to the soup by mistaking your stratum for the floor or the ceiling.

What this looks like, practically, is the sort of work that the soup-dwellers on either side find frustrating. The physicist who sat with quantum measurement until it broke his pictures, and declined to replace them with a marketable new picture. The phenomenologist who reduced and reduced until she hit something she could not further reduce, and declined to christen it. The contemplative who declines to produce quotable theses because theses are precisely the objects that become soup. They are not being coy. They are declining to add more structure to a middle that already has too much. The soup rewards thesis-production. It does not reward the maintenance of open questions.

Both sides of the great two-direction project will continue. The tower will keep rising; the shaft will keep descending. Neither will arrive at the terminus that would collapse them into each other, because that terminus is not an arrival but the condition of probing itself, and probing does not end, except by the accident of a particular inquirer's death. What accumulates between the two projects — what we work within, and think within, and inherit, and pass on — is the middle. And the middle is made of us. Of our closures and our prematurities, our stratified successes and our residual abandoned loves, the things we declared true and the things we quietly stopped saying. The soup is not something we are stuck in from the outside; it is the sediment of our own probing, and everyone working on either end is also, simultaneously, feeding it.

The discipline is to feed it as little as necessary. To keep the loops open longer than is comfortable. To perform the second perception. To know that your stratum is not the floor and not the ceiling. To walk — because the walking is the whole of what is available. Neither arrival nor abandonment. Sustained, disciplined, second-perception-performing probing, held open against the constant and entirely understandable temptation to close early and produce thesis.

The roads do all lead somewhere, including the road that runs directly away. The arrival is not an arrival. The stopping is the failure mode. The walking is the whole of what is available.

The middle is made of us. That is not a complaint. It is the condition.
